Tutoring for ADHD and Neurodivergent Students: What Works

Updated April 2025
Beth Burgin

Every student learns differently — but for students with ADHD, autism, or other neurodivergent profiles, traditional teaching methods may fall short. Specialized tutoring can help these learners thrive by honoring their unique strengths and providing structured, compassionate support tailored to how they learn best.

Understanding Neurodivergence

Neurodivergence is a term that includes a range of cognitive differences such as ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, dyslexia, and more. These students may process information differently, experience attention and memory challenges, or require adjustments to communication and pacing. The key to successful tutoring is recognizing that different doesn’t mean deficient.

Common Learning Challenges

Students with ADHD and other neurodivergent profiles may struggle with:

  • Maintaining focus for extended periods
  • Following multi-step directions
  • Processing written or verbal information quickly
  • Executive function skills like organization, time management, and planning
  • Social communication, especially in group settings

Tutoring sessions can address these challenges by creating structure, breaking down tasks, and offering a more individualized learning environment.

What Works in Tutoring for Neurodivergent Students

1. Predictable Routines and Structure

Neurodivergent students often thrive with routines. Tutors should start sessions the same way each time, provide clear agendas, and stick to consistent pacing. Predictability builds trust and reduces anxiety.

2. Flexibility and Responsiveness

While structure is important, so is flexibility. A skilled tutor can adapt mid-session if the student is overwhelmed or needs to shift strategies. Sessions should honor the student’s needs that day — not just the agenda.

3. Use of Visual Aids and Tools

Many neurodivergent learners benefit from visual supports like diagrams, color-coding, charts, and timers. These help clarify information and support memory and attention.

4. Encouragement Over Correction

Rather than emphasizing mistakes, tutors should focus on effort and progress. Positive reinforcement and strengths-based feedback help build self-confidence — a critical part of long-term academic success.

5. Built-In Breaks and Movement

Short breaks can dramatically improve focus. For students who are kinesthetic learners or who experience attention fatigue, built-in movement or brain breaks can reset focus without sacrificing learning time.

How Parents Can Support the Process

Parents play a crucial role in supporting neurodivergent learners:

  • Communicate openly with the tutor about what works and what doesn’t
  • Celebrate progress over perfection
  • Ensure the home learning environment is structured and distraction-free
  • Help students build routines for homework and review

Support That Fits

Neurodivergent students are not broken — they simply need environments that support how they learn best. When tutors understand and adapt to a student’s brain, it creates space for growth, joy, and lasting academic confidence.
