Building Strong Reading Comprehension Skills

Updated April 2025
Beth Burgin

Reading comprehension is one of the most critical academic skills a student can develop. It affects not only success in language arts, but also performance in science, social studies, and even math word problems. If your child struggles with understanding what they read, this article will guide you through practical strategies to help improve their reading comprehension step-by-step.

Why Reading Comprehension Is So Important

Strong readers don’t just sound out words — they understand the ideas behind the text. Reading comprehension allows students to draw conclusions, identify key themes, make inferences, and connect concepts. Without it, even fluent readers can fall behind in school.

Signs Your Child May Be Struggling with Comprehension

  • Can read aloud but can't explain what they've read
  • Struggles to answer questions about characters or events
  • Skips over confusing parts without pausing
  • Dislikes reading or avoids it altogether

Top Strategies to Build Reading Comprehension Skills

1. Preview the Text

Encourage your child to look at titles, headings, and illustrations before diving in. This activates prior knowledge and sets a purpose for reading.

2. Ask Questions Before, During, and After

Good readers ask themselves questions like “What might happen next?” or “Why did the character do that?” This keeps them engaged and thinking critically.

3. Use Active Reading Techniques

  • Highlight key details
  • Underline unfamiliar words
  • Write margin notes or summaries
  • Pause to paraphrase after each paragraph

4. Build Vocabulary in Context

When children encounter unfamiliar words, encourage them to guess the meaning from context clues. Then look up definitions together to reinforce learning.

5. Practice Summarizing

Ask your child to explain what they read in their own words. This reveals how much they truly understood and helps with information retention.

6. Encourage Visualization

Have your child create mental pictures or draw scenes from the text. Visualization improves memory and helps students emotionally connect with the material.

Creating a Positive Reading Environment

Set up a quiet, cozy space for daily reading time. Offer a variety of reading materials — fiction, nonfiction, graphic novels — and let your child help choose what they read to keep interest high.

When to Seek Outside Support

If your child continues to struggle with comprehension despite these strategies, professional support can make a lasting difference. A tutor can provide personalized reading interventions based on your child’s specific challenges.

Schedule a session today to help your child build confidence, improve understanding, and enjoy reading again.
